# V1 Product Direction: Flower Finder

## What this document is for

This is the product direction for V1 and a reference point for future decisions. It describes the customer problem, the experience we want to create, the boundaries of the first release, and the principles that should continue to guide the product.

It is intentionally not a technical implementation plan. Code structure, frameworks, deployment, test plans, and task breakdowns belong in separate working documents.

## V1 delivery posture

This phase prioritizes build speed, learning speed, and short feedback loops. The team should be able to change the product direction quickly as the flower-shop workflow, content, and customer experience become clearer.

For now:

- Automated tests are not part of the V1 scope.
- Formal visual verification and screenshot-review passes are not part of the V1 scope.
- Product decisions should favor simple, reversible choices that are easy to change.
- Polish that does not improve customer learning or the florist handoff should wait.
- A complete, usable discovery-to-handoff journey matters more than broad feature coverage.

This is a deliberate early-stage tradeoff, not a permanent quality policy. Testing and structured visual review should be reconsidered once the core flow stabilizes, multiple people depend on the product, or regressions begin slowing iteration.

## Product idea

People often recognize the flowers they are drawn to before they know the names, meanings, prices, or combinations that would help them make a purchase. Flower Finder turns visual preference into useful language a customer can take to a florist.

The central product transformation is:

> Simple visual choices become a clear, personal starting point for a florist conversation.

The product should help someone move from “I like these” to “I’m looking for something soft, garden-inspired, and blush-toned, around this budget, for this person and occasion.”

## The customer job

When choosing flowers, a customer should be able to:

- Browse real flowers and arrangements without needing expert vocabulary.
- Save the flowers and visual qualities they genuinely like.
- Understand the patterns in those choices in plain language.
- See a small set of relevant possibilities rather than an overwhelming catalog.
- Bring a concise preference brief to a florist and continue the conversation there.

The product assists judgment; it does not make the purchase decision for the customer or replace the florist’s expertise.

## Who V1 is for

V1 is for a flower-shop customer who:

- Has an occasion or recipient in mind, but may not know exactly what to ask for.
- Responds more easily to images than to a long list of flower names.
- Wants inspiration and confidence before speaking with a florist.
- May have a budget, color preference, or practical constraint.

The florist is also a primary stakeholder. The output must be useful behind the counter, not just attractive on a screen.

## The V1 experience

### 1. A calm invitation

The opening should make the value immediately understandable:

> Find the flowers that feel right.

The tone is warm, editorial, and encouraging. It should feel like guided discovery, not a personality test, quiz, or sales funnel.

### 2. Lightweight context

Before or during discovery, the customer can optionally share:

- Who the flowers are for.
- The occasion.
- A comfortable budget range.
- Whether they want individual flowers, a bunch, a bouquet, an arrangement, or florist guidance.

These questions should reduce uncertainty without making the customer complete a form before seeing anything interesting. Skipping is always acceptable.

### 3. Visual discovery

The heart of V1 is a focused browse-and-respond experience. Show one prominent flower or arrangement at a time, with enough information to support a quick choice:

- Real photograph.
- Name, and Japanese name where relevant and verified.
- Price or starting price with a clear unit.
- A short, human description.
- A few useful visual descriptors.
- Availability when it affects the decision.

Customers can like, skip, favorite, undo, or open more detail. These actions should be obvious and available through visible controls. Gestures may make the experience feel natural, but they must never be the only way to use it.

The customer should be able to reach a meaningful result after a small number of choices. Reviewing the full catalog is not the goal.

### 4. A transparent preference reflection

After enough choices, show a concise reflection of what the customer selected. Use language such as:

- “You seem drawn to…”
- “Your selections suggest…”
- “You frequently chose…”

Reflect observable preferences: colors, shapes, textures, mood, arrangement style, and flowers. Do not infer personality, identity, relationships, or psychological traits.

The reflection should feel useful and recognizable, not like a hidden score being revealed. Customers should be able to revise choices and see the reflection change.

### 5. A small set of recommendations

Offer three to five flowers or arrangements that connect clearly to the customer’s choices and context. Every recommendation should answer “Why this?” in plain language.

Recommendations should account for:

- The customer’s selected visual qualities.
- Occasion and recipient, when provided.
- Budget, when provided.
- What is actually available or seasonally realistic.
- The fact that a florist may adapt the final selection.

V1 should favor relevance and explainability over volume. A shorter list that a customer can discuss is more valuable than a broad ranking.

### 6. A small symbolic layer

Hanakotoba-inspired meaning can add emotional resonance, but it is secondary to the practical recommendation. Meanings should be short, gentle, and clearly presented as associations rather than universal truths.

Use language such as:

> Commonly associated with gratitude and gentle affection.

Always acknowledge that meanings vary by flower, color, culture, and source. Do not make symbolic content predictive, mystical, or definitive. Do not invent meanings to fill gaps.

### 7. The florist handoff

The end product is a shareable, readable preference brief—not a checkout flow. It should summarize:

- Occasion and recipient.
- Budget and purchase format.
- Preferred colors, shapes, styles, and textures.
- Favorite flowers.
- Characteristics the customer skipped or wants to avoid, only when there is enough evidence.
- A few recommended directions.
- An optional personal note.

The customer should be able to show, copy, print, or revise this summary locally. The wording should invite collaboration:

> Here’s what I’m drawn to. What would you suggest from what’s available today?

## Product principles

### Show, then explain

Lead with the visual experience. Introduce names, terms, and meanings after the customer has something concrete to react to.

### Personal, not prescriptive

Reflect preferences without pretending to know the person. The product should help customers describe themselves, not define them.

### Useful to a real florist

Every recommendation and summary should be actionable in a shop. Commercial reality—availability, seasonality, budget, and substitutions—matters more than a perfect catalog description.

### Explain the connection

Customers should understand why a flower or arrangement was recommended. Avoid black-box authority and unexplained rankings.

### Keep discovery light

The experience should create momentum. Optional context, a small number of choices, and a clear next step are preferable to exhaustive setup.

### Keep the product easy to change

V1 should remain small enough that the team can revise content, sequencing, recommendation language, and the florist handoff without a long stabilization cycle. Avoid product commitments that make early experiments expensive to reverse.

### Treat uncertainty honestly

Prices, stock, seasonality, botanical details, and flower meanings can change or vary. Label estimates and mock inventory clearly, and let the florist have the final word.

### Make the handoff the success moment

The product succeeds when the customer feels more confident starting a florist conversation—not when they spend the longest time browsing.

## V1 content and inventory direction

V1 should use a deliberately curated set of real flowers and representative arrangements rather than trying to model every flower available in the world.

The initial set should be broad enough to reveal meaningful preference patterns across:

- Soft and vivid colors.
- Layered, rounded, spiky, airy, and structured forms.
- Romantic, natural, elegant, cheerful, and restrained styles.
- Individual stems, bunches, bouquets, and arrangements.

Commercial fields should come from the flower shop wherever possible:

- Shop name and naming conventions.
- Current or clearly dated price guidance.
- Pricing unit.
- Availability and seasonal limits.
- Suitable occasions and bouquet combinations.
- Fragrance, vase life, and care guidance where the shop can verify them.

When shop data is incomplete, mark content as sample or illustrative. Sample data must be easy for the team to identify and replace; it must not look like a promise of current stock or price.

## Image direction: real photography only

The product will use actual flower and arrangement photography. We are not planning to generate replacement images after the fact, and generated images should not be used to make the inventory feel more complete.

Image priority:

1. Photographs supplied by the flower shop of flowers it actually sells.
2. Rights-cleared photographs with verified source, creator, and license information for temporary or supplemental coverage.
3. A smaller catalog when a suitable, permissioned photograph is unavailable.

Authenticity is more important than catalog breadth. A real image that reflects the shop’s flowers is more valuable than a visually perfect image of a flower the shop may not carry.

For every image, maintain enough provenance to answer:

- What flower or arrangement does this represent?
- Who supplied or created the photograph?
- Where did it come from?
- Do we have permission to use it?
- Was it cropped or otherwise modified?

Do not use screenshots or copied images from social media, search results, Pinterest, other florists, or marketplaces without explicit permission. Image credits may be shown in a small credits area when appropriate, but provenance should be retained even when attribution is not legally required.

Photography should feel coherent across the experience: clear subject, minimal clutter, consistent crop, and enough detail for customers to notice color, shape, and texture. Do not artificially imply that all images were photographed in the same studio when they were not.

## Recommendation and reflection philosophy

V1 should be understandable and predictable at a product level. A recommendation is a strong match because of visible choices and stated context, not because of an opaque claim about the customer.

The product should:

- Give extra weight to favorites because they signal stronger intent.
- Recognize repeated colors, shapes, textures, and styles.
- Use skipped flowers as soft evidence, not hard rejection, unless the customer clearly marks something to avoid.
- Use occasion, budget, and availability as practical filters or explanations.
- Prefer a diverse set of recommendations over several near-duplicates.
- Make it possible to explain each result in one sentence.

This direction should remain valid if the underlying ranking method changes in the future.

## Trust, language, and cultural care

- Use plain, non-judgmental language.
- Avoid claims that flowers reveal personality or compatibility.
- Avoid presenting symbolic meanings as fixed facts.
- Distinguish flower names, cultural associations, and botanical facts.
- Have Japanese-language and hanakotoba content reviewed by someone qualified when it is included.
- Do not overstate scientific or care information when sources disagree.
- Make it clear when stock, price, or availability needs confirmation with the florist.

## What V1 is—and is not

V1 is:

- A visual preference discovery tool.
- A lightweight bridge between customer taste and florist language.
- A curated recommendation experience grounded in real inventory where possible.
- A local, no-account way to prepare for a flower-shop conversation.

V1 is not:

- A personality assessment or psychological profile.
- A replacement for a florist’s judgment.
- A marketplace, cart, checkout, or delivery service.
- A real-time inventory guarantee.
- A universal flower encyclopedia.
- A social network or public profile product.
- A runtime AI image or meaning generator.

## Success signals for V1

The most useful early signals are qualitative and behavioral:

- Customers reach a preference summary without needing to inspect everything.
- Customers recognize themselves in the summary and can correct it easily.
- Customers understand why recommendations appeared.
- Customers use or say they would use the handoff with a florist.
- Florists say the summary gives them a useful starting point rather than extra translation work.
- Customers can distinguish sample guidance from confirmed shop availability and price.

The first release should optimize for confidence and conversation quality, not maximum session length or the number of flowers displayed.

## Future directions

These are opportunities, not V1 commitments:

- Shop-specific catalogs that reflect live seasonal availability.
- Florist editing of recommendations or the final preference brief.
- Better arrangement-level discovery, not only single-flower discovery.
- Customer notes, saved briefs, and private links for sharing with a recipient.
- Occasion-specific starting points and culturally sensitive guidance.
- Feedback loops from florists about substitutions, availability, and successful recommendations.
- More nuanced preference controls for color intensity, formality, fragrance, and maintenance.
- Accessibility improvements for customers who do not primarily browse through images.
- Measurement of which visual attributes and explanations genuinely help customers decide.

Future features should preserve the core promise: make it easier for a customer and a florist to understand each other.

## Decision checklist for future work

Before adding a feature or expanding the catalog, ask:

1. Does this help the customer describe what they want?
2. Does it make the florist conversation more useful?
3. Is the information grounded in real, reviewable content?
4. Does the customer understand why the product made this suggestion?
5. Are uncertainty, cultural variation, price, and availability represented honestly?
6. Does the feature preserve a calm, low-effort discovery experience?

If the answer to several of these is no, it is probably outside the V1 direction—or it needs a clearer product rationale before it is pursued.
